# 01 — Product Strategy

## 1. Product Thesis

Feady's proved the demand: Moroccan and Francophone small businesses want digital menus, online ordering, and a professional web presence without complexity. But Feady's — and every tool like it — built the product like a feature checklist. The result is cluttered, generic, and feels like cheap POS software skinned for the web.

Quickarte bets on a different lever: **product taste as competitive advantage.**

The insight is that in underserved markets, the bar isn't just "does it work" — it's "does it make me look serious." A café owner in Casablanca choosing between a cluttered dashboard and a tool that feels like Stripe doesn't need convincing. The premium feel IS the feature.

We win by being:
- **Faster to first value** — menu live in under 5 minutes, not 30
- **Radically simpler** — 5 screens instead of 25, opinionated defaults instead of configuration mazes
- **Visually superior** — the merchant dashboard and the customer menu both look better than anything in the market
- **Mobile-native for operations** — built for the owner checking orders from behind the counter, not sitting at a desk

This isn't about more features. It's about fewer features done at a level of quality that makes switching feel like an upgrade in professional identity.

---

## 2. Wedge Market

**First 100 customers:**
- **Geography:** Casablanca, Rabat, Marrakech, Tangier — urban Morocco
- **Business type:** Independent cafés and casual restaurants (not fine dining, not street food carts)
- **Size:** 1–3 locations, 2–15 staff, owner-operated
- **Behavior:** Already on Instagram to attract customers. May have tried Feady's or Glovo but found them either too complex, too ugly, or too expensive. Uses WhatsApp for informal orders. Wants to look modern to younger clientele.
- **Language:** French-first, Arabic support expected, Darija a bonus
- **Tech comfort:** Uses smartphone daily, can install apps, but has never configured a SaaS dashboard

**Why this wedge:** These owners are already spending energy on digital presence (Instagram posts, WhatsApp order management). They're actively looking for something better. They care about how their business appears online. They'll switch for a product that makes them look premium with zero effort.

## 3. Positioning Statement

Quickarte is the commerce OS for restaurants that care how they look. One tool to publish your menu, take orders, and run your service — designed to feel as premium as the experience you serve. No clutter, no training, live in minutes.

## 4. ICPs

### ICP 1: "Karim" — The Ambitious Café Owner
- Runs a modern café in Casablanca, 25–40 years old
- Opened 1–3 years ago, growing but operationally messy
- Posts on Instagram, takes WhatsApp orders, prints paper menus
- Wants to look professional and modern to his younger clientele
- Pain: Juggling WhatsApp, paper, and cash feels chaotic and unscalable
- Success: Menu is online, orders come in digitally, he checks everything from his phone between tables

### ICP 2: "Nadia" — The Restaurant Manager
- Manages a family-owned restaurant in Rabat, 30–45 years old
- Not the owner but runs daily operations
- Needs to update the menu when items run out, manage table flow, track what sold today
- Pain: Current tools (paper + phone calls) are slow and error-prone
- Success: She can 86 an item in 2 taps, see today's orders at a glance, and print a QR code for each table

### ICP 3: "Youssef" — The Multi-Unit Operator
- Owns 2–3 locations across cities, 35–50 years old
- Needs visibility across locations without being physically present
- Currently uses a mix of POS, WhatsApp groups, and Excel
- Pain: No single view of what's happening across his businesses
- Success: One dashboard, all locations, real-time order and revenue view from his phone

## 5. Jobs to Be Done

### Functional
- "When I open for the day, I want to see what needs my attention right now, so I can act instead of searching."
- "When a customer sits down, I want them to scan a QR and see my menu instantly, so I don't need printed menus."
- "When I run out of an ingredient, I want to mark an item unavailable in 2 taps, so customers don't order it."
- "When an order comes in, I want to hear it, see it, and confirm it from my phone, so nothing gets missed."
- "When I close for the night, I want to see what I sold today without pulling reports."

### Emotional
- "I want to feel like I have a real system, not a pile of workarounds."
- "I want my digital menu to make my place look as good as it actually is."
- "I want to stop feeling embarrassed when customers ask if we take orders online."

### Social
- "I want customers to think my business is modern and well-run."
- "I want to share my menu link on Instagram and have it look professional."
- "I want other business owners to ask me what tool I use."

## 6. Core Value Proposition

**Quickarte gives you a professional digital storefront and order management system that's live in 5 minutes, runs from your phone, and makes your business look premium — with zero training and zero commission.**

## 7. Differentiation Levers (Rank-Ordered)

1. **Design taste and product quality** — Every screen looks and feels better than any competitor. This is the #1 reason someone screenshots it and shares it.

2. **Speed to first value** — Menu published and QR code generated in under 5 minutes. Not "easy setup" — actually fast setup with opinionated defaults.

3. **Mobile-first operations** — Not a desktop dashboard crammed into mobile. Built from scratch for the owner standing behind the counter.

4. **Simplicity through opinion** — We don't give you 40 settings. We give you the right default and let you override later. Progressive disclosure, not feature soup.

5. **Customer-facing experience quality** — The menu page, the ordering flow, the mini-website — all feel premium. The merchant's customers notice the difference.

6. **Francophone-native** — Not a translated English product. French-first, Arabic-ready, Darija-aware. Cultural fit matters for trust.

7. **Zero-commission model** — Direct sales, no marketplace tax. Merchant keeps everything.

## 8. Risks and Tradeoffs

### High Risk
- **Distribution in Morocco is relationship-driven.** Product quality alone won't create adoption. We'll need feet-on-the-ground sales, café-to-café onboarding, or a viral referral mechanic. The product must be so visually striking that owners want to show it off.
- **Payment infrastructure is fragmented.** Morocco has CMI, cash-on-delivery dominance, and limited card penetration. We must support cash orders from day one and not gate value behind online payment setup.

### Medium Risk
- **"Too premium" can feel foreign.** If the product looks too Western/Silicon Valley, it might feel like it's not "for them." The design must feel premium AND locally trustworthy — not cold.
- **Feady's has first-mover relationships.** They've already onboarded merchants. Switching costs are low (menus are easy to rebuild) but inertia is real.
- **Scope creep toward retail.** The temptation to generalize early (clothing stores, jewelers) will dilute the restaurant-first story. Resist until the restaurant wedge is locked.

### Tradeoffs We're Making
- **Fewer features, better product.** We will lose deals to competitors who have loyalty programs, advanced CRM, and multi-warehouse. That's fine. We're not competing on feature count.
- **Opinionated defaults over flexibility.** Some merchants will want custom layouts, complex pricing rules, or deep configuration. We'll say no in v1. The product is for the 80%, not the 20% who want a configurable platform.
- **Restaurant-first means saying no to retail.** The data model will support it later, but the UX, onboarding, and copy will be restaurant-specific. We can't serve two masters at launch.
- **Mobile-first means desktop is secondary.** The merchant app is designed for phone. A desktop version will exist but won't be the primary design target. This is a feature, not a limitation.
