# 01 — Product Strategy

## 1. Product Thesis

Morocco's independent urban food merchants — boulangeries, cafés, brunch spots, and salons de thé — are moving online through a patchwork rather than a coherent operating layer. Instagram creates demand, WhatsApp captures pre-orders, QR codes are familiar, and a legacy POS may sit at the counter, but the merchant still lacks one calm system that feels worthy of the brand they are building.

Quickarte bets on a different lever: **product taste and modularity as competitive advantage.**

The insight is that owner-operated merchants do not want a configurable platform. They want a calm, opinionated tool that makes their business look and feel serious from the first day. A shop owner in Maarif, Gueliz, Agdal, or Tangier choosing between a generic back office and a tool that feels premium does not need a feature-count argument. The premium feel IS the feature — and charging only for the modules they actually use turns "premium" from aspiration into affordability.

We win by being:
- **Faster to first value** — menu live in under 5 minutes, not 30
- **Radically simpler** — 5 screens instead of 25, opinionated defaults instead of configuration mazes
- **Visually superior** — the merchant dashboard and the customer menu both look better than the category norm
- **Mobile-native for operations** — built for the owner standing behind the counter at 7am, not sitting at a desk
- **Modular by pricing** — Menu+QR, Online Ordering, Loyalty, and Analytics are independently priced; a merchant only pays for what they turn on

This is not about more features. It is about fewer features done at a level of quality that makes switching feel like an upgrade in professional identity, with MAD pricing that matches the module in use.

## 2. Wedge Market

**First 100 customers:**
- **Geography:** Casablanca, Rabat, Marrakech, Tangier
- **Business type:** Independent boulangeries, small cafés, brunch spots, and salons de thé. Not chains, not fine dining, not dark kitchens.
- **Size:** 1 location, 2-8 staff, owner on the floor daily
- **Behavior:** Active on Instagram for storefront marketing. Often taking orders through WhatsApp or Instagram DMs, sometimes running a legacy POS that does not connect to the online customer journey. Accepts cash and in-store terminal payments; increasingly asked for QR menus and click-and-collect by regulars.
- **Language:** French. Moroccan urban food merchants operate in French daily; this is fact, not stylistic preference. fr-MA is the only active locale for pilot.
- **Tech comfort:** Uses a smartphone and a tablet daily, comfortable with Instagram, QR codes, and card terminals, but has never configured a SaaS dashboard end-to-end.

**Why this wedge:** Independent Moroccan boulangeries, cafés, and salons de thé are squeezed between rising costs, Instagram-driven expectations, and fragmented software habits. They care deeply about how their shop presents. They are the perfect audience for a beautifully designed, modular tool that replaces the patchwork and charges only for what they use.

**Why now:** Smartphone penetration, QR-code familiarity, and click-and-collect habits all accelerated after COVID. Loi 09-08 / CNDP expectations are mature enough that privacy posture matters, but the Moroccan F&B SaaS field is still thin. The tooling layer is ready; the UX layer is not.

**Why us:** Design taste, mobile-first operations, and modular pricing are all underweight in the field. A small, opinionated team can ship the product the incumbents and category tools will not — they are too broad to be this opinionated.

## 4. ICPs

### ICP 1: "Hassan" — Artisan Boulangerie, Maarif, Casablanca
- Runs a neighborhood boulangerie with his brother; both are on the floor daily
- Currently takes pre-orders manually on WhatsApp and answers the same questions repeatedly during service
- Has no real software beyond a counter setup and Instagram
- Pain: Regulars ask if they can reserve bread, viennoiseries, or a cake for pickup, but every order becomes a fragile WhatsApp thread.
- Success: A QR menu in the shop, a click-and-collect link in his Instagram bio, and an order queue he can check from his phone without changing how customers pay.

### ICP 2: "Salma & Karim" — Brunch Café, Gueliz, Marrakech
- Couple running a 20-cover brunch café with 4-5 staff
- Uses a legacy POS that does not talk to anything else
- Instagram is active, but the customer journey after discovery is inconsistent
- Pain: The counter system, online presence, and reporting all live in separate places. They do not trust the weekly numbers without manually checking them.
- Success: One branded storefront, one merchant workspace, and weekly analytics they trust enough to make menu and staffing decisions.

### ICP 3: "Younes" — Neo-Boulangerie / Café Hybrid, Agdal, Rabat
- Opened 12-18 months ago with an Instagram-driven brand
- Runs a hybrid boulangerie/café format with a small team and a design-conscious customer base
- Uses a patchwork of tools, including an off-brand checkout or ordering page
- Pain: The digital customer journey looks weaker than the physical shop. Orders, menu edits, loyalty, and analytics are scattered across tools.
- Success: A storefront that matches his brand, a simple order capture flow, and a modular system he can expand without rebuilding the stack.

## 5. Jobs to Be Done

### Functional
- "When I open for the day, I want to see what needs my attention right now, so I can act instead of searching."
- "When a customer sits down, I want them to scan a QR and see my menu instantly, so I don't need printed menus."
- "When a regular asks if they can pre-order a tarte, I want to send them a link, so I stop losing those orders."
- "When I run out of an ingredient, I want to mark an item unavailable in 2 taps, so customers don't order it."
- "When an order comes in, I want to hear it, see it, and confirm it from my phone, so nothing gets missed."
- "When I close for the night, I want to see what I sold today — and how it compares to last week — without pulling reports."
- "When a regular comes back for the tenth time, I want to reward them automatically, so loyalty isn't another thing I have to remember."

### Emotional
- "I want to feel like I have a real system, not a pile of workarounds."
- "I want my digital menu to look as good as my shop does in real life."
- "I want to stop feeling embarrassed when a regular asks if we have click-and-collect."

### Social
- "I want my customers to think my shop is modern and well-run."
- "I want to share my menu link on Instagram and have it look as considered as my feed."
- "I want other boulangers and café owners to ask me what tool I use."

## 7. Differentiation Levers (Rank-Ordered)

1. **Design taste and product quality** — Every screen looks and feels better than the category norm. This is the #1 reason an owner screenshots it and shares it with a neighbor.

2. **Modular pricing and entitlements** — Merchants subscribe to the exact subset of modules they need (Menu+QR / Online Ordering / Loyalty / Analytics). No forced bundles. No feature we don't use that pads the bill.

3. **Speed to first value** — Menu published and QR code generated in under 5 minutes. Opinionated defaults, not a setup wizard maze.

4. **Mobile-first operations** — Not a desktop dashboard crammed into mobile. Built from scratch for the owner standing behind the counter.

5. **Simplicity through opinion** — We don't give you 40 settings. We give you the right default and let you override later. Progressive disclosure, not feature soup.

6. **Customer-facing experience quality** — The QR menu, the click-and-collect flow, the mini-website — all feel premium. The merchant's customers notice the difference.

7. **Morocco-native, Loi 09-08-aware** — French-native copy that matches Moroccan business norms, TVA-aware totals, fr-MA defaults, Africa/Casablanca time, and CNDP-ready privacy posture from day 1.

## 8. Competitors — Honest Read

### Feadys
- **Strength:** Direct local reference for digitizing merchant menus and customer-facing ordering surfaces in Morocco. Familiar category language, locally relevant positioning, and a clearer fit than foreign generic tools.
- **Weakness:** The category still leaves room for a more premium merchant workspace, stronger modular entitlements, and a more opinionated owner-operated workflow.
- **Why we still win:** We are building a calm operating layer, not only a customer-facing menu surface. Our wedge is premium storefront + order capture + loyalty + analytics, with each module independently priced and designed as one coherent system.

### Category Landscape
The rest of the competitive pressure is category-level rather than a single named incumbent: legacy POS tools with limited online ordering, ad-hoc WhatsApp/Instagram ordering with no infrastructure, and a long tail of unbranded online-menu builders. These options are familiar and cheap, but they do not give an owner one branded, trustworthy system for the daily operating loop.

### Summary
The category wins on familiarity and low switching effort. It loses on **design taste, modularity, and mobile-native operations**. That is a durable wedge for a small, opinionated team — provided we resist the pull toward feature parity.

## 9. Risks and Tradeoffs

### High Risk
- **Moroccan F&B SaaS adoption is thin.** Merchant education on paying for SaaS is harder than in more mature markets. We need a concrete wedge: dense neighborhood sales, Instagram-visible storefronts, and founder-led onboarding that proves value quickly.
- **We are not a payments processor.** Some merchants will ask us to take orders AND collect money. We will say no in v1 and rely on out-of-band settlement, which matches the dominant Moroccan retail norm.

### Medium Risk
- **"Too premium" can feel abstract.** If the product reads like a generic design tool, an owner will not recognize the day-to-day shop workflow in it. Design must feel premium AND understand boulangerie/café work — warmth in copy, not coldness.
- **Incumbent inertia.** A merchant with any existing counter system has catalog, staff habits, and daily routines embedded there. Switching cost is real even if the online layer is weak. We need a migration story (CSV import at minimum) early.
- **Module-bloat within modules.** Modular pricing is a feature; module-internal feature creep is the same disease at a smaller scale. Each module must stay opinionated.

### Tradeoffs We're Making
- **Fewer features, better product.** We will lose deals to vendors with kitchen display, multi-warehouse, and advanced CRM. That's fine. We're not competing on feature count.
- **Opinionated defaults over flexibility.** Some merchants will want custom layouts, complex pricing rules, or deep configuration. We'll say no in v1.
- **Boulangerie/café/salon de thé-first means saying no to full-service restaurants.** The data model can support adjacent formats later, but the UX, onboarding, and copy will focus on the wedge at launch. We can't serve two masters at launch.
- **Mobile-first means desktop is secondary.** The merchant app is designed for phone. A desktop version will exist but won't be the primary design target. This is a feature, not a limitation.
- **fr-MA only at pilot.** ar-MA scaffolding stays on disk and is surfaced post-pilot after an RTL audit. This keeps copy, support, and product QA focused while preserving the path to Arabic.
