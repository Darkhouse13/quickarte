# 01 — Product Strategy

## 1. Product Thesis

France's independent food merchants — boulangeries, cafés, brunch spots — are served by a crowded field of incumbents: Sunday, Zelty, Innovorder, Lightspeed, Square. Each solves part of the job, but the category as a whole has built for feature parity instead of product quality. Owners end up stitching together a POS, a QR tool, and an online-ordering product, paying three bills for an experience that still feels dated.

Quickarte bets on a different lever: **product taste and modularity as competitive advantage.**

The insight is that owner-operated merchants don't want a configurable platform — they want a calm, opinionated tool that makes their business look and feel serious. A boulangère in the 11e choosing between a Zelty dashboard and a tool that feels like Stripe doesn't need convincing. The premium feel IS the feature — and charging only for the modules she actually uses turns "premium" from aspiration into affordability.

We win by being:
- **Faster to first value** — menu live in under 5 minutes, not 30
- **Radically simpler** — 5 screens instead of 25, opinionated defaults instead of configuration mazes
- **Visually superior** — the merchant dashboard and the customer menu both look better than anything in the market
- **Mobile-native for operations** — built for the owner standing behind the counter at 7am, not sitting at a desk
- **Modular by pricing** — Menu+QR, Online Ordering, Loyalty, and Analytics are independently priced; a merchant only pays for what they turn on

This isn't about more features. It's about fewer features done at a level of quality that makes switching feel like an upgrade in professional identity, at a price point that matches the module in use.

## 2. Wedge Market

**First 100 customers:**
- **Geography:** Paris (intra-muros, especially 10e–20e and inner suburbs), Lyon (1er–7e), Bordeaux (centre + Chartrons)
- **Business type:** Independent boulangeries and small cafés / brunch spots. Not chains, not fine dining, not dark kitchens.
- **Size:** 1 location, 2–8 staff, owner on the floor daily
- **Behavior:** Active on Instagram for storefront marketing. Often running a legacy POS (Zelty, Lightspeed, Square) plus a separate QR or click-and-collect tool. Accepts CB and cash; increasingly asked for online ordering by regulars.
- **Language:** French only. No English, no translation layer needed for v1.
- **Tech comfort:** Uses a smartphone and a tablet daily, comfortable with Instagram and CB terminals, but has never configured a SaaS dashboard end-to-end.

**Why this wedge:** Independent French boulangeries and small cafés are squeezed between rising costs and fragmented software stacks. They already pay €40–€150/month across two or three tools. They care deeply about how their shop presents — aesthetics are a core part of the brand. They're the perfect audience for a beautifully designed, modular tool that replaces the patchwork and charges only for what they use.

**Why now:** Post-COVID, QR menus and click-and-collect became table stakes. RGPD matured. Stripe's coverage in France is now first-class for both Connect and Billing. The tooling layer is ready; the UX layer is not.

**Why us:** Design taste, mobile-first operations, and modular pricing are all underweight in the incumbent field. A small, opinionated team can ship the product the incumbents won't — they're too big to be this opinionated.

## 4. ICPs

### ICP 1: "Camille" — Artisan Boulangère, Paris 11e
- 34, owner of a 2-year-old artisan boulangerie near rue Oberkampf
- Runs the shop with her partner and two apprentis; on the floor from 6am
- Currently uses a Lightspeed POS at the counter and a basic Wix page for the website; no online ordering

- Pain: Regulars keep asking if they can pre-order a pain de campagne or a tarte for pickup. She has no good answer. Lightspeed's online module is ugly and expensive.
- Success: A QR on every table for brunch, a click-and-collect link she can drop in her Instagram bio, and a dashboard she can check from her phone between batches.

### ICP 2: "Léa & Julien" — Brunch Café, Lyon 2e
- Mid-30s couple, 4-year-old brunch spot in presqu'île, 20 covers
- 6 staff including themselves; they rotate floor and back-office roles
- Currently on Zelty for the POS and Sunday for QR pay-at-table; no loyalty tool, no analytics they trust
- Pain: Paying two subscriptions for two tools that don't talk to each other. Zelty's reporting feels like an Excel export. Sunday's customer-facing QR page looks like everyone else's.
- Success: One branded storefront, one merchant app, a simple stamp card for regulars, and a weekly analytics view that tells them what actually sold — without a spreadsheet.

### ICP 3: "Mehdi" — Neo-Boulangerie, Bordeaux Chartrons
- 41, opened a hybrid boulangerie/café 14 months ago
- 5 staff; he works the counter in the morning and does ops/admin in the afternoon
- Currently on Square Terminal + a standalone click-and-collect tool he found on a Facebook group
- Pain: The click-and-collect tool has no analytics, no loyalty, and the checkout page breaks his brand. Square's dashboard is functional but generic.
- Success: A storefront that matches his Instagram aesthetic, a loyalty program he can enable in one click, and a weekly email summarizing revenue, top items, and peak hours.

## 5. Jobs to Be Done

### Functional
- "When I open for the day, I want to see what needs my attention right now, so I can act instead of searching."
- "When a customer sits down, I want them to scan a QR and see my menu instantly, so I don't need printed menus."
- "When a regular asks if they can pre-order a tarte, I want to send them a link, so I stop losing those orders."
- "When I run out of an ingredient, I want to mark an item unavailable in 2 taps, so customers don't order it."
- "When an order comes in, I want to hear it, see it, and confirm it from my phone, so nothing gets missed."
- "When I close for the night, I want to see what I sold today — and how it compares to last week — without pulling reports."
- "When a regular comes back for the tenth time, I want to reward them automatically, so loyalty isn't another thing I have to remember."

### Emotional
- "I want to feel like I have a real system, not a pile of workarounds."
- "I want my digital menu to look as good as my shop does in real life."
- "I want to stop feeling embarrassed when a regular asks if we have click-and-collect."

### Social
- "I want my customers to think my shop is modern and well-run."
- "I want to share my menu link on Instagram and have it look as considered as my feed."
- "I want other boulangers and café owners to ask me what tool I use."

## 7. Differentiation Levers (Rank-Ordered)

1. **Design taste and product quality** — Every screen looks and feels better than any competitor. This is the #1 reason an owner screenshots it and shares it with a neighbor.

2. **Modular pricing and entitlements** — Merchants subscribe to the exact subset of modules they need (Menu+QR / Online Ordering / Loyalty / Analytics). No forced bundles. No feature we don't use that pads the bill.

3. **Speed to first value** — Menu published and QR code generated in under 5 minutes. Opinionated defaults, not a setup wizard maze.

4. **Mobile-first operations** — Not a desktop dashboard crammed into mobile. Built from scratch for the owner standing behind the counter.

5. **Simplicity through opinion** — We don't give you 40 settings. We give you the right default and let you override later. Progressive disclosure, not feature soup.

6. **Customer-facing experience quality** — The QR menu, the click-and-collect flow, the mini-website — all feel premium. The merchant's customers notice the difference.

7. **French-native, RGPD-native** — French-first copy, French business norms (tickets, TVA-aware totals), RGPD-ready (consent, export, deletion) from day 1. Not a translated product bolted onto a US core.

## 8. Competitors — Honest Read

### Sunday
- **Strength:** Strong brand, good funding, clean QR pay-at-table UX, real adoption in France.
- **Weakness:** Narrow scope — pay-at-table is most of it. Weak on catalog, weak on loyalty, not an operating tool for the owner. Customer-facing QR pages are templated and look the same across merchants.
- **Why we still win:** We're a full, modular operating system, not a payment QR. Our storefront is distinctive per merchant; theirs is not.

### Zelty
- **Strength:** Deep POS, established in French restaurants, good hardware integrations.
- **Weakness:** Heavy, configuration-driven, dated UX. Reporting feels like Excel. Mobile experience is an afterthought. Pricing is bundled and opaque.
- **Why we still win:** We're mobile-first, opinionated, and modular. A boulangère who wants Menu+QR+Loyalty shouldn't have to buy a full POS suite to get them.

### Innovorder
- **Strength:** Enterprise-leaning, covers chains and multi-site, integrated kitchen flows.
- **Weakness:** Built for chains. The onboarding and config surface is wildly over-scoped for a single-location boulangerie. Design is generic enterprise SaaS.
- **Why we still win:** They can't credibly serve the owner-operator wedge — it's too small for their sales motion and too simple for their product.

### Lightspeed
- **Strength:** Global reach, broad POS feature set, hardware ecosystem.
- **Weakness:** Generic, not France-specific, expensive, online/loyalty modules feel bolted on. The dashboard is a feature checklist, not a product.
- **Why we still win:** France-native copy and norms, a single coherent product, modular pricing, and design quality they can't match at their scale.

### Square
- **Strength:** Hardware-first, clean onboarding, strong brand in small-merchant segments.
- **Weakness:** Fundamentally an American product. French-language polish is thin. Loyalty and online modules look generic. RGPD posture is minimal.
- **Why we still win:** We're built for France from the first commit. Our defaults, copy, and compliance posture match the market; theirs are translations.

### Summary
The incumbents win on features and distribution. They lose on **design taste, modularity, and mobile-native operations**. That is a durable wedge for a small, opinionated team — provided we resist the pull toward feature parity.

## 9. Risks and Tradeoffs

### High Risk
- **Distribution in France is crowded.** Every boulangerie already has a vendor relationship. We need a concrete wedge — either door-to-door in dense neighborhoods (Oberkampf, Chartrons, presqu'île) or a viral artifact (a storefront so good it gets shared on Instagram).
- **Payment infrastructure is mature but contested.** Stripe Connect gives us great defaults, but some merchants have existing acquirer relationships (BNP, CA, SumUp, Zettle). We need the payment abstraction to let us slot in alternatives over time without rewriting checkout.

### Medium Risk
- **"Too premium" can feel abstract.** If the product reads like a Linear clone, a 55-year-old boulanger won't recognize himself in it. Design must feel premium AND feel like it understands boulangerie work — warmth in copy, not coldness.
- **Incumbent inertia.** A merchant on Zelty for 4 years has 4 years of catalog, staff, and habits in there. Switching cost is real even if the data is portable. We need a migration story (CSV import at minimum) early.
- **Module-bloat within modules.** Modular pricing is a feature; module-internal feature creep is the same disease at a smaller scale. Each module must stay opinionated.

### Tradeoffs We're Making
- **Fewer features, better product.** We will lose deals to incumbents who have kitchen display, multi-warehouse, and advanced CRM. That's fine. We're not competing on feature count.
- **Opinionated defaults over flexibility.** Some merchants will want custom layouts, complex pricing rules, or deep configuration. We'll say no in v1.
- **Boulangerie/café-first means saying no to full-service restaurants.** The data model will support them later, but the UX, onboarding, and copy will be boulangerie/café-specific. We can't serve two masters at launch.
- **Mobile-first means desktop is secondary.** The merchant app is designed for phone. A desktop version will exist but won't be the primary design target. This is a feature, not a limitation.
- **French only in v1.** We will lose international inquiries. Worth it — it keeps copy, support, and compliance sharp.
