# 01 — Product Strategy

## 1. Product Thesis

Morocco's independent urban fast-food restaurants and cafés are moving online through a patchwork rather than a coherent operating layer. Instagram creates demand, WhatsApp and phone calls capture orders, QR codes are familiar, and a legacy POS may sit at the counter, but the merchant still lacks one calm system that feels worthy of the business they are building.

Quickarte bets on a different lever: **product taste, modularity, and customization fluency as competitive advantage.**

The insight is that owner-operated merchants do not want a configurable platform. They want a calm, opinionated tool that makes their business look and feel serious from the first day while handling the real operational shape of Moroccan fast food and cafés: tacos with size, meat, sauces, fries, and toppings; shawarmas with variants; paninis, burgers, juices, coffee, and breakfast items that change by service. A shop owner in Maarif, Gueliz, Agdal, or Tangier choosing between a generic back office and a tool that feels premium does not need a feature-count argument. The premium feel IS the feature — and charging only for the modules they actually use turns "premium" from aspiration into affordability.

We win by being:
- **Faster to first value** — menu live in under 5 minutes, not 30
- **Radically simpler** — 5 screens instead of 25, opinionated defaults instead of configuration mazes
- **Visually superior** — the merchant dashboard and the customer menu both look better than the category norm
- **Mobile-native for operations** — built for the owner standing behind the counter at 11pm, not sitting at a desk
- **Customization-fluent** — product options and variants are native to the catalog and customer ordering flow, not hacked into item descriptions
- **Modular by pricing** — Menu+QR, Online Ordering, Loyalty, and Analytics are independently priced; a merchant only pays for what they turn on

Feadys remains the primary direct competitor reference. This is not about more features. It is about fewer features done at a level of quality that makes switching feel like an upgrade in professional identity, with MAD pricing that matches the module in use.

## 2. Wedge Market

**First 100 customers:**
- **Geography:** Casablanca, Rabat, Marrakech, Tangier
- **Business type:** Independent fast-food restaurants and cafés. Not chains, not fine dining, not dark kitchens.
- **Size:** 1 location, 2-8 staff, owner on the floor daily
- **Behavior:** Heavily driven by Instagram, WhatsApp, phone orders, and walk-up traffic. Fast-food merchants almost always need customization on each item: size, meat, sauces, toppings, extras, and notes. Cafés care about a polished Instagram-to-menu journey, table QR menus, and click-and-collect for quick meals and drinks.
- **Language:** French. Moroccan urban food merchants operate in French daily; this is fact, not stylistic preference. fr-MA is the only active locale for pilot.
- **Tech comfort:** Uses a smartphone and a tablet daily, comfortable with Instagram, QR codes, and card terminals, but has never configured a SaaS dashboard end-to-end.

**Why this wedge:** Independent Moroccan fast-food restaurants and cafés are squeezed between rising costs, Instagram-driven expectations, phone-order chaos, and fragmented software habits. They need the same primitives immediately: a branded menu, QR access, clean order capture, loyalty, and a workspace that is simple enough for service. They are the perfect audience for a beautifully designed, modular tool that replaces the patchwork and charges only for what they use.

**Why now:** Smartphone penetration, QR-code familiarity, and click-and-collect habits all accelerated after COVID. Loi 09-08 / CNDP expectations are mature enough that privacy posture matters, but the Moroccan F&B SaaS field is still thin. The tooling layer is ready; the UX layer is not.

**Why us:** Design taste, mobile-first operations, customization-aware catalog structure, and modular pricing are all underweight in the field. A small, opinionated team can ship the product the incumbents and category tools will not — they are too broad to be this opinionated.

## 3. Positioning Statement

Quickarte is the commerce OS for independent Moroccan fast-food restaurants and cafés that want a branded menu, clean order capture, loyalty, and weekly visibility without adopting a complex POS. One calm, premium, modular tool — live in minutes, run from a phone, fluent in tacos, shawarmas, paninis, breakfasts, drinks, and the options customers actually choose. ## 4. ICPs

### ICP 1: "Yassine" — Fast-Food Restaurant, Maarif, Casablanca
- Runs a fast-food shop specializing in French tacos, shawarmas, and kebabs
- Open until late, with high-volume weekend nights and a service rhythm built around phone calls, walk-up orders, and takeaway
- Mostly takeaway, with a few stools at a counter
- Pain: Instagram-DM customers ask for the menu and disappear because there is no clean link to share; phone orders are error-prone when every tacos needs size, meat, sauces, and extras.
- Success: A branded menu link in Instagram bio, product options that make customization clear, and an order queue he can check from his phone without changing how customers pay.

### ICP 2: "Imane" — Neighborhood Café, Gueliz, Marrakech
- Runs a café serving petits déjeuners, paninis, salads, drinks, and coffee
- Has 6 indoor tables and 4 sidewalk seats, with a morning pickup rush and steady in-place service
- Uses printed menus, WhatsApp messages, and manual notes for table and pickup requests
- Pain: Customers ask for the menu before arriving, table menus get outdated, and breakfast pickups are handled manually during the busiest part of the day.
- Success: QR menus on each table, a click-and-collect link for breakfast pickups, and a simple catalog she can update from her phone before service.

### ICP 3: "Adam" — Snack Bar / Café Hybrid, Agdal, Rabat
- Runs a mixed eat-in and takeaway snack bar serving burgers, tacos, paninis, juices, and coffee
- Uses a legacy POS at the counter that does not connect to anything online
- Wants to keep the physical workflow while improving the customer-facing journey
- Pain: The counter system, online presence, and reporting all live in separate places. Orders, menu edits, loyalty, and weekly numbers require manual reconciliation.
- Success: One branded storefront, one merchant workspace, and weekly analytics he trusts enough to make menu and staffing decisions.

## 7. Differentiation Levers (Rank-Ordered)

1. **Design taste and product quality** — Every screen looks and feels better than the category norm. This is the #1 reason an owner screenshots it and shares it with a neighbor.

2. **Modular pricing and entitlements** — Merchants subscribe to the exact subset of modules they need (Menu+QR / Online Ordering / Loyalty / Analytics). No forced bundles. No feature they don't use that pads the bill.

3. **Customization-fluent catalog** — The product handles tacos with multiple sauces, two meats, size choices, toppings, extras, and notes cleanly without forcing the merchant into a complex POS configuration model.

4. **Speed to first value** — Menu published and QR code generated in under 5 minutes. Opinionated defaults, not a setup wizard maze.

5. **Mobile-first operations** — Not a desktop dashboard crammed into mobile. Built from scratch for the owner standing behind the counter.

6. **Simplicity through opinion** — We don't give you 40 settings. We give you the right default and let you override later. Progressive disclosure, not feature soup.

7. **Morocco-native, Loi 09-08-aware** — French-native copy that matches Moroccan business norms, TVA-aware totals, fr-MA defaults, Africa/Casablanca time, and CNDP-ready privacy posture from day 1.

## 8. Competitors — Honest Read

### Feadys
- **Strength:** Direct local reference for digitizing merchant menus and customer-facing ordering surfaces in Morocco. Familiar category language, locally relevant positioning, and a clearer fit than foreign generic tools.
- **Weakness:** The category still leaves room for a more premium merchant workspace, stronger modular entitlements, and a more opinionated owner-operated workflow.
- **Why we still win:** We are building a calm operating layer, not only a customer-facing menu surface. Our wedge is premium storefront + order capture + loyalty + analytics, with each module independently priced and designed as one coherent system.

### Delivery Aggregators
Glovo and Jumia Food are tangential, not direct competitors. They own delivery rails and marketplace demand; Quickarte owns the merchant's branded storefront, QR/menu experience, order-capture surface, loyalty loop, and operating workspace. Some pilot merchants will use aggregators in parallel, and v1 does not replace them.

### Category Landscape
The rest of the competitive pressure is category-level rather than a single named incumbent: legacy POS tools with limited online ordering, ad-hoc WhatsApp/Instagram ordering with no infrastructure, and a long tail of unbranded online-menu builders. These options are familiar and cheap, but they do not give an owner one branded, trustworthy system for the daily operating loop or a clean options model for heavily customized items.

### Summary
The category wins on familiarity and low switching effort. It loses on **design taste, modularity, customization fluency, and mobile-native operations**. That is a durable wedge for a small, opinionated team — provided we resist the pull toward feature parity.

## 9. Risks and Tradeoffs

### High Risk
- **Moroccan F&B SaaS adoption is thin.** Merchant education on paying for SaaS is harder than in more mature markets. We need a concrete wedge: dense neighborhood sales, Instagram-visible storefronts, and founder-led onboarding that proves value quickly.
- **Fast-food merchants are SaaS-price-sensitive.** We must prove ROI within the first month or churn. The product has to connect directly to fewer missed orders, faster order capture, repeat purchases, or visible customer experience improvement.
- **We are not a payments processor.** Some merchants will ask us to take orders AND collect money. We will say no in v1 and rely on out-of-band settlement, which matches the dominant Moroccan retail norm.

### Medium Risk
- **"Too premium" can feel abstract.** If the product reads like a generic design tool, an owner will not recognize the day-to-day shop workflow in it. Design must feel premium AND understand fast-food/café work — warmth in copy, not coldness.
- **Customization can become configuration debt.** Fast-food options are essential, but the product must avoid turning into a full POS rules engine. We need a tight model that covers the common cases cleanly.
- **Incumbent inertia.** A merchant with any existing counter system has catalog, staff habits, and daily routines embedded there. Switching cost is real even if the online layer is weak. We need a migration story (CSV import at minimum) early.
- **Module-bloat within modules.** Modular pricing is a feature; module-internal feature creep is the same disease at a smaller scale. Each module must stay opinionated.

### Tradeoffs We're Making
- **Fewer features, better product.** We will lose deals to vendors with kitchen display, multi-warehouse, and advanced CRM. That's fine. We're not competing on feature count.
- **Opinionated defaults over flexibility.** Some merchants will want custom layouts, complex pricing rules, or deep configuration. We'll say no in v1.
- **Fast-food + café-first means saying no to full-service tableside fine dining.** The data model can support adjacent independent restaurant formats later, but the UX, onboarding, and copy will focus on the wedge at launch. We can't serve two masters at launch.
- **Mobile-first means desktop is secondary.** The merchant app is designed for phone. A desktop version will exist but won't be the primary design target. This is a feature, not a limitation.
- **fr-MA only at pilot.** ar-MA scaffolding stays on disk and is surfaced post-pilot after an RTL audit. This keeps copy, support, and product QA focused while preserving the path to Arabic.
